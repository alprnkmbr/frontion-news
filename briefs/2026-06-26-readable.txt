# Hormuz Evacuation Halted After Ship Attack, Europe's Heatwave Turns Lethal, and the Supreme Court Reshapes US Immigration
Date: 2026-06-26

A cargo ship struck by a projectile in the Strait of Hormuz froze the UN's evacuation of 11,000 stranded sailors, underscoring that the US-Iran deal has not yet made the waterway safe. Meanwhile, a record heatwave is killing people across Europe, and the US Supreme Court delivered a pair of rulings that will transform immigration enforcement.
---

## 1. Hormuz: Deal or No Deal, the Strait Is Still Dangerous
The UN's International Maritime Organization paused its planned evacuation of more than 11,000 sailors stranded in the Strait of Hormuz after a cargo ship transiting the waterway was struck by what the UK Maritime Trade Operations agency described as 'an unknown projectile.' The Singapore-flagged vessel Ever Lovely was hit 7.5 nautical miles southeast of Oman's port of Dahit on Thursday but continued its transit without requiring assistance, according to maritime risk firm Vanguard. US officials told American media that Iran fired on the ship.IMO chief Arsenio Dominguez said the vessel had not been operating under the IMO's evacuation framework and that the operation would remain paused until 'necessary safety guarantees' were clarified. Iran's Persian Gulf Strait Authority warned that vessels using unauthorized routes would not be guaranteed safe passage. The message was blunt: transit only on Tehran's terms, or accept the risk.This happened just four days after the IMO announced the evacuation effort with the cooperation of Iran, Oman, and the US, and nine days after the US-Iran MOU set a 60-day negotiation window. Oil prices had briefly fallen below $72.48 a barrel — the pre-war level — before edging back up to $73.23 after the attack. The price signal is clear: markets are pricing in the deal's success, but the waterway itself is still contested ground.

Why it matters: The MOU promised safe passage, but Thursday's attack and Iran's route warnings show that control of Hormuz remains a live leverage point. The deal is on paper, not yet on the water.

## 2. Trump's $87 Billion War Bill Faces a Divided Congress
The White House formally asked Congress to approve $87.6 billion, mostly for 'urgent needs' connected to the Iran war — $67 billion for the Pentagon, including $21 billion for munitions and $12.1 billion for classified programs. The request also bundles $11 billion for farmers and $1.4 billion for an Ebola response in Central Africa.The request landed a day after the Senate passed a symbolic war powers resolution demanding Trump halt or seek authorization for the Iran campaign — the first resolution of its kind to clear Congress. Five Republican senators defied the president. One of them, Bill Cassidy of Louisiana, reportedly had a shouting match with Trump at a closed-door lunch, telling him 'you have not told the American people what's going on' and that the war was supposed to last four weeks, not four months.By end of day Wednesday, Cassidy reversed course after receiving briefings from Vice President JD Vance and envoy Steve Witkoff, voting to defeat a second war powers measure. Trump branded the Republican dissenters as 'losers.' The Pentagon's own chief financial officer has put the war's cost so far at $29 billion, though defense analysts and lawmakers say that figure understates the full financial damage.

Why it matters: Trump can probably get the money, but the political damage is mounting. A four-month war with no clear end state is eroding congressional tolerance, and the $87 billion ask will force a public accounting the White House has avoided.

## 3. Europe's Heatwave Is Now a Public Health Emergency
France raised its health alert to the highest level as record temperatures continued to break across the continent. Paris banned public alcohol consumption and takeaway sales during peak heat hours after the city's ambulance service reported four times the normal rate of cardiac arrests over 24 hours. A three-year-old child was found dead in a car in the Paris region, and the head of the Rennes emergency department linked five or six deaths in people's homes to the extreme temperatures. 'It's not just the very elderly; it's people aged 60 and up,' he said.Germany is bracing for 40C on Friday, with overnight temperatures in the southwest matching a national heat record set in 2019. The national rail operator is offering free ticket cancellations. The Czech Republic issued extreme weather warnings. Luxembourg hit 38.3C, its highest June temperature ever. Three French nuclear plants went offline because river cooling water was too warm.UN climate chief Simon Stiell said 'Europe's savage heatwave has the fingerprints of the climate crisis all over it.' Paris mayor Emmanuel Grégoire called it irresponsible for joggers to be on the streets, and France's health minister Stéphanie Rist warned that young people were suffering cardiac arrests, not just the elderly. Italy expects temperatures over 40C on Monday, and Florence's Uffizi museum halted ticket sales because its air conditioning could not cope. The extreme conditions are shifting east, with Germany, Austria, and the Czech Republic in the firing line next.

Why it matters: This is not a slow-moving climate trend — it is killing people right now. The combination of infrastructure failure (nuclear plants offline, hospitals saturated) and the widening demographic impact suggests Europe's heat preparedness has not caught up with the new baseline.

## 4. Supreme Court Greenlights End of Protected Status for 350,000 Haitians
In a pair of 6-3 rulings, the Supreme Court handed the Trump administration two major immigration victories. The first allows the government to strip Temporary Protected Status from roughly 350,000 Haitians and 6,100 Syrians who have lived and worked in the US for years — some since the 2010 earthquake. Justice Samuel Alito wrote that the law governing TPS clearly prevents courts from reviewing government decisions, and that Haitian migrants were unlikely to prove the administration's actions were racially discriminatory.The second ruling held that migrants seeking asylum at the US-Mexico border are not entitled to apply until they set foot on US soil — effectively reviving the 2016 'metering' policy that the Biden administration had rescinded. Justice Sonia Sotomayor dissented, warning 'more people will die' as a result. Justice Elena Kagan called the TPS decision racially motivated, noting Trump's campaign amplification of false rumors about Haitian immigrants.James Percival, general counsel for the Department of Homeland Security, said the ruling proved 'the T in TPS stands for temporary.' Jill Habig of Public Rights Project, which filed amicus briefs on behalf of 47 local governments, said the decision 'puts hundreds of thousands of people at risk' and will produce 'a community crisis' as families face deportation to countries experiencing violence and humanitarian collapse.

Why it matters: These rulings remove the last legal barriers to mass removals. The TPS decision in particular will cascade beyond Haiti and Syria — it opens the door to terminating protected status for other national groups and shifts the constitutional balance on judicial review of immigration decisions.

## 5. Venezuela's Quakes Expose the Cost of Decades of Infrastructure Decay
Two earthquakes of magnitude 7+ struck Venezuela on Wednesday, killing at least 235 people and injuring more than 4,300. The death toll is still rising as rescuers search collapsed buildings. The quakes hit a country where, as BBC analysis notes, the housing stock has deteriorated for years due to cement shortages caused by the collapse of the state-owned cement industry after nationalization under Hugo Chávez. Badly needed repairs to buildings have gone unmade, making them far more vulnerable to collapse.Interim president Delcy Rodríguez — installed after US forces seized Nicolás Maduro in a January raid — declared a state of emergency and tasked a general with leading the response. In a notable shift from Maduro's policies, Rodríguez publicly accepted offers of help from the Trump administration and right-wing governments in Chile, the Dominican Republic, and El Salvador. She also recently replaced the general running the housing ministry with a civilian architect and the general heading the electricity ministry with an electrical engineer — acknowledging, implicitly, that two decades of military appointments degraded state capacity.The damage to Venezuela's already fragile infrastructure is immense. Communication channels to the worst-affected areas were down for hours after the quakes, partly because independent media outlets had been shut down under Maduro. The country's emergency services, chronically underfunded, are struggling to cope.

Why it matters: The quakes turned Venezuela's long-running governance crisis into an acute humanitarian disaster. Rodríguez's willingness to accept US and allied aid marks a real break from the Maduro era, but the response is being led by the same military establishment whose mismanagement contributed to the vulnerability in the first place.

---

## Bottom Line
The US-Iran deal exists on paper, but Thursday's attack on a cargo ship in the Strait of Hormuz — and the immediate pause of the UN's sailor evacuation — revealed how far it is from translating into safe passage. Tehran is enforcing its own rules for the waterway and firing on vessels that don't comply. In Washington, Trump's $87 billion war funding request meets a Congress where even his own party is publicly fracturing over the campaign's length and cost. In Europe, the heatwave has crossed from uncomfortable to lethal, with cardiac arrest surges in Paris and nuclear plants shutting down. And in a single day, the Supreme Court removed legal protections from hundreds of thousands of immigrants while making it harder for new asylum seekers to even apply. The common thread: systems under stress — diplomatic, political, environmental, institutional — are not bending. They are breaking.
